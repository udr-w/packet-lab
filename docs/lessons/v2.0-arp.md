## Session narrative (2026-07-04)

### Warm-up and theory

Warm-up question bridged from v1.2: what does `via` in `ip route get` mean?
Student answered correctly (destination beyond the local network), refined to:
`via` = a next hop the packet must be handed to; no `via` = direct on-link
delivery (physical transmission and "needs a next hop" are separate things).

Theory covered before any code, per teaching order:

- **Two address layers**: IP (logical, hierarchical, routed, end-to-end) vs
  MAC (physical, flat, one-hop, what the NIC hardware filters on).
- **Frame** = the layer-2 envelope carrying src/dst MAC; rebuilt at every hop
  while the IP packet inside travels end-to-end.
- **The gap ARP fills**: routing gives the next hop's IP; the frame needs its
  MAC; nothing else ever provided it.
- Request is **broadcast** (`ff:ff:ff:ff:ff:ff`) out of necessity — you cannot
  unicast a question to an address you don't know. Reply is unicast.

Student built a full mental model unprompted (ICMP inside IP inside frame,
all four addresses placed correctly) and asked what happens when DNS has no
answer — deferred to Version 3 with a short complete answer: ping sends
nothing until the name resolves.

### Observing the neighbour cache

`ip neigh` on the student's machine showed three states live:

- `192.168.8.1 ... REACHABLE` — the router, recently confirmed.
- `192.168.8.152 ... STALE` — the phone from v1.1, expired but kept.
- `169.254.169.254 dev virbr0 FAILED` — asked, nobody ever answered.

Student prediction (correct, with correct reasoning): the cache holds only
devices actually framed packets to — no inventory of the LAN.

Student then spotted the STALE hazard unprompted: after a reconnect a phone
may get a different IP, so a stale entry could point at the wrong device.
Discussed the engineering trade-off: broadcast-per-packet (always right,
wasteful) vs trust-forever (fast, eventually wrong) vs Linux's middle path —
use the stale answer but immediately re-verify with a **unicast** probe.

### The live experiment

Student deleted the router's entry and pinged 8.8.8.8 while the assistant
captured (`tcpdump -i wlp0s20f3 -nn -e -l 'arp or (icmp and host 8.8.8.8)'`).
Prediction first: whose MAC does the ARP request ask for? Student answered
router's, not 8.8.8.8's — correct: ARP resolves the next hop, never the
final destination.

Evidence (timestamps matched student's terminal to the millisecond):

- `ip neigh del` at 13:21:04 → broadcast `who-has 192.168.8.1 tell
  192.168.8.173` at 13:21:04.568 → unicast reply `is-at 98:a9:42:13:87:32`
  at 13:21:04.576 — **8 ms** question to answer.
- The ICMP frames that followed: **dst MAC = router, dst IP = 8.8.8.8** —
  two-layer addressing in one capture line.
- In a full hour of capture the laptop broadcast **exactly twice**, once per
  forced deletion. Everything else was quiet unicast maintenance.
- "How can I know the delete worked, if `ip neigh show` says REACHABLE right
  after?" → the entry was re-created within milliseconds because background
  traffic (this very AI session) immediately re-asked. The broadcast on the
  radio is the proof the cache was empty; `ip monitor neigh` streams state
  transitions live.

Student then reproduced the whole exchange in the extended viewer (their own
terminal, their own tool): 6 packets — their forced broadcast + reply, plus
the router unicast-probing *them* twice (cache maintenance runs in both
directions). Stats: 1 broadcast / 5 unicast.

Bonus real-world observation: the phone reconnected during the lesson and
came back as `192.168.8.118`, not `.152` — the exact DHCP-reassignment
scenario the student had asked about, observed for real.

### Why ARP never crosses the router

ARP frames are ethertype `0x0806` — not IP, no TTL, nothing to route — and
are addressed to the broadcast MAC, which means "everyone on this segment"
by definition. Routers do not forward broadcasts. LAN-scoped by design;
resolving only the next hop is the same fact seen from the other side.

## Tooling work this session

- **ARP mode added to the viewer**: `python3 scripts/packetlab.py arp`.
  Capture filter `arp` with `-e` (frame headers — the MACs are the story),
  `ArpPacket` parser, ARP table + stats (requests/replies, broadcast/unicast
  split — the REACHABLE-vs-STALE story made countable).
- **Bug found & fixed during verification**: tcpdump's final blank stdout
  line on shutdown was counted as an "unparsed packet"; blank lines now
  dropped in `capture.iter_lines`.
- **Self-describing instrument**: stats panel shows the exact tcpdump
  command it is running (`Running :` line).
- **Human-readable labels**: every MAC/IP shown carries its meaning beside
  the raw value — `(me)` from `/sys/class/net/<iface>/address`, `(router)`
  from the default route, device names (`Udara-s-S24-Ultra`) via reverse DNS
  against the router's DHCP-registered names, `(broadcast: everyone on this
  LAN)`. Raw value never replaced. Nothing hardcoded.
